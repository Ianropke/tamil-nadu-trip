Two Weeks through Tamil Nadu and Sri Lanka

From private kitchens in Chennai and temples along the Coromandel Coast to the mansions of Chettinad, the lively streets of Madurai, and the green landscapes of Sri Lanka.

This won't be a holiday where we just look at places through a car window. We are going to cook in private homes, eat off banana leaves, walk through old merchant quarters, and stay in everything from colonial townhouses to historic mansions and jungle retreats. Along the way, we will encounter people, food culture, crafts, and stories that cannot be experienced from home.

History during the journey

What we encounter along the way is no accident

The temples, the harbor cities, the French street names, the large mansions of Chettinad, and Sri Lanka's religious landscape are all traces of thousands of years of trade, faith, migration, and the struggle for control of the Indian Ocean.

On this journey, we move through three historical layers: the ancient Tamil kingdoms, the European colonial era, and the modern societies that emerged after independence. History is not just found in museums. It lives on in the language, the food, the architecture, the rituals, and in the way people understand their identity.

South India & Sri Lanka

1. Tamil Nadu before the Europeans

An ancient cultural region – not just southern India

Tamil Nadu is home to one of the world's oldest continuously surviving literary and linguistic traditions. Tamil belongs to the Dravidian language family and is fundamentally different from Hindi. Before the Europeans arrived, the region was shaped by the Pallava, Chola, and Pandya dynasties, leaving lasting marks on temple architecture, art, and trade.

The Chola Empire emerged around the Kaveri River and became one of South Asia's strongest maritime power between the 9th and 13th centuries, with trade links to Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia.

What we will see:
  • Mahabalipuram's cave temples (Pallava dynasty)
  • Gangaikonda Cholapuram (Chola Empires storhed)
  • Madurai (Pandya Kingdom and the living temple city)
  • Chettinad's mansions (trade networks across Asia)
Before the colonial era, Tamil Nadu was already part of an international world of trade, seafaring, art, and political power.

2. Why Religion Plays Such a Major Role

Temples are more than historical monuments

The great temples are not ruins, but active social and religious centers with daily rituals, processions, music, and offerings. In Tamil Nadu, Shiva, Vishnu, Murugan, and the Goddess are central (like Meenakshi in Madurai).

The Aadi month: Falls in July/August and is associated with goddess worship, the life-giving monsoon waters of the rivers, and ceremonies (like Aadi Perukku, which gives thanks for the arrival of water).

What we will see:
  • Flowers, colors, and incense already in the streets of Chennai
  • The evening ritual in Madurai (Palliyarai Pooja)
  • The difference between Hindu temples in Tamil Nadu and Buddhist shrines in Sri Lanka
The temple continues to function as a living social, religious, and economic hub.

3. Tamil Identity & the Connection across the Sea

Language, regional pride, and Sri Lanka

Tamil identity is built primarily on language, literature, and history. Tamil Nadu has its own food culture, film industry, and strong regional self-awareness alongside the shared Indian identity.

The connection to Sri Lanka: The Palk Strait is narrow, and for centuries, language, religion, and people have moved across it. Sri Lanka's Tamil-speaking communities have deep roots, and the British later brought additional labor from South India to work on the plantations.

What we will see:
  • The prominent place of language in films, signs, and everyday life
  • How proximity to South India shapes Sri Lanka's language, food, and politics
  • The historical ties linking the island to the mainland
Tamil Nadu and Sri Lanka are connected – throughout history, the sea has both connected and divided them.

After landing, we will take it easy and adapt to the warm South Indian climate. On our first evening, we'll head straight to a private Tamil home to cook local dishes together.

Why does it look like this?

Chennai: Why is the city located here?
Chennai did not grow around a natural harbor, but because of its location along the trade routes of the Bay of Bengal. The British built Fort St. George in the 1600s, and the city expanded around the fort, trading posts, and pre-existing Tamil settlements. This is why Chennai contains a mix of ancient temples, colonial neighborhoods, busy markets, and a modern metropolis.

Today's Three Highlights

Mahabalipuram

Monolith Temples & Coastal Culture

Coastal town known for ancient rock-cut temples, stone carvers, and beaches.

Why does it look like this?

Mahabalipuram: Why were temples carved out of the rock?
The free-standing temples (Five Rathas) and reliefs were carved directly from the natural granite cliffs in the 7th and 8th centuries under the Pallava kings. These were not just artistic feats, but political and religious monuments demonstrating the crown's control over the coastline and key trade routes crossing the Bay of Bengal.

Puducherry

French Charm and Coastal Life

We arrive in the former French colony of Puducherry, where colonial townhouses, cafes, temples, and boutiques sit side-by-side, offering a gentler pace than the rest of the journey.

Why does it look like this?

Puducherry: Why is it called White Town?
Puducherry was France's premier colony in India from the 1670s. The district closest to the sea was planned as the European quarter and later named White Town. Behind the bright facades and straight streets lies a history of trade, colonial power, and physical separation from the Tamil quarter.

👁️ Keep an eye out for: How the houses, street width, and daily street life change as we cross between White Town and the Tamil quarter.

Chettinad

From the Coast to the Mansions of Chettinad

We drive toward Chettinad, stopping along the way at Gangaikonda Cholapuram, one of the grand temples of the Chola Empire.

Why does it look like this?

Chettinad: The mansions were built by trade – not by kings
The Chettiar families built extensive trade and financial networks across South and Southeast Asia (including Burma and Ceylon) during the 19th and 20th centuries. Their earnings and design ideas were brought home to Chettinad and channeled into huge family mansions built around local courtyards, utilizing Burmese teak and materials sourced from distant trading hubs. This is why the mansions feel both Tamil and international.

👁️ Keep an eye out for: The international materials used in Chettinad family mansions and the multiple courtyards built to capture the breeze.

Madurai

An Evening in Madurai

We arrive in Madurai, where grand temples and lively streets shape the city.

Why does it look like this?

Madurai: Why is the temple the absolute center of the city?
Madurai is one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities, and its streets are laid out in a mandala pattern (concentric squares) radiating directly from the giant Meenakshi Amman Temple. The temple is not just a historical monument, but the beating heart of the city, where trade, ceremonies, music, and social life blend together around the clock.

👁️ Keep an eye out for: The fact that the temple in Madurai is not set apart from the town – the entire city grew outwards in concentric circles from it.

Highlights

Sri Lanka

Rocks, Jungle, and Quiet Mornings

The final days of the trip unfold at a greener, gentler pace. Lakes, jungles, wild elephants, rocky peaks, and time to enjoy our surroundings await.

Why does it look like this?

Sri Lanka: What connects the island with Tamil Nadu?
For two millennia, Sri Lanka has developed its own kingdoms, Buddhist traditions (such as Theravada Buddhism), and cultural landscapes. However, the Palk Strait is narrow, and ties to the mainland have always been close. Portuguese, Dutch, and British arrivals also left their mark on the country's history, with the latter recruiting Tamil workers from South India for the tea estates. The country's recent history includes the civil war (which ended in 2009), which is important to keep in mind to understand contemporary dynamics.

👁️ Keep an eye out for: The difference between the Hindu temple spaces in Tamil Nadu and the Buddhist shrines (like the Dambulla cave temple) in Sri Lanka.

Practical Advice & Health

Advice to keep healthy and ensure a comfortable holiday.

🧼

Health & Food Hygiene

Hand sanitizer is your most important tool. To avoid stomach issues, follow this practical advice:

✅ Safe to eat & drink:
  • Freshly made, steaming hot food (such as freshly made idli, dosa, and parotta).
  • Vegetarian food from busy restaurants (e.g. Murugan Idli Shop).
  • Fresh coconut water directly from coconuts opened in front of you.
  • Only bottled water from sealed bottles (also for brushing teeth).
❌ Avoid completely:
  • Buffet food and dishes that have been sitting lukewarm for a long time.
  • Raw salads and unpeeled fruit (except bananas you peel yourself).
  • Ice cubes in drinks at local cafes and street corners.
  • Tap water and unsealed water bottles.
🦟

Monsoon & Nature Conditions

Evening showers during the monsoon can be pleasantly refreshing, but be aware of increased mosquito activity after rain in coastal towns and the Sri Lankan jungle. Always have a good mosquito repellent at hand.

⚠️ Nature & Rocks: Always follow the guide's instructions during safari (e.g., in Minneriya National Park) and watch out for slippery rocks when climbing Pidurangala Rock after rain.

🛺

Transport & Getting Around

Traffic in India and Sri Lanka is hectic and intense. When riding in an auto-rickshaw, it's a good idea to agree on the price beforehand and hold on tight during sharp turns in narrow streets.

⚠️ Dining Times: Arrive early at popular lunch spots (like The Bangala in Chettinad) to secure a table and avoid the busiest times in the middle of the day.
